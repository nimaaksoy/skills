# Overused Words and Phrases for Lyrics

This is a list of words and phrases that have become red flags for generic, AI-typical lyric writing. They are not strictly forbidden — most of them have legitimate concrete uses. The job of this list is to make the assistant pause and check whether a word is being used as a *concrete sensory detail* or as a *vague emotional placeholder*.

The list applies **only to lyrics**, not to style prompts. Style prompts may use any of these words freely (e.g., "neon" is fine in a style prompt for synthwave; the question only arises in the lyrical content itself).

## How to apply this list

When generating a lyric, treat any word or phrase from this list as a signal to check the line. Ask:

- Am I using this word to describe an actual concrete thing the listener can picture in a specific moment? (Legitimate — proceed.)
- Am I using this word as an abstract emotional placeholder, gesture, or mood? (Cliché — rewrite.)

Example — `shadows`:
- Legitimate concrete use: "The shadows from the kitchen light fell across her face" — specific, visual, grounded in a moment.
- Cliché use: "Lost in the shadows of my heart" — abstract, emotional, gestural.

Example — `river`:
- Legitimate: "We waded the river in our jeans till our pockets filled with stones" — specific scene.
- Cliché: "A river runs through my soul" — abstract metaphor.

When in doubt, rewrite. Use these words only when the concrete sensory case clearly applies and no equally specific replacement is available.

## Application rules

- The list applies to all forms of each word — plurals, possessives, conjugations, and altered spellings. If "echo" is on the list, the same scrutiny applies to "echoes," "echoed," and "echoing."
- When rewriting, do not reach for an adjacent generic word (replacing "shadows" with "darkness," or "neon" with "bright"). The fix is replacing abstraction with specific sensory detail. See `lyric-craft.md` for the full replacement strategy.
- Apply the same scrutiny in fictional dialogue and stylistic exceptions as in standard lyric writing.
- Do not mention this list to the user unless they explicitly ask.
- A word from this list may appear in lyrics when it functions as concrete sensory detail rather than abstract emotion. The user does not need to give permission for legitimate concrete uses.
