# Study guide — what brilliant Persian lyric-writing looks like

The fastest way to learn Persian lyric craft is to study songs that already work. This file points at specific tracks worth analysing and names the **technique** in each one — what to listen *for*, not what to copy.

Lyrics are reproduced under fair-use commentary only as needed; for the full lyric, listen to the source.

---

## How to study a song

For each track on this list:

1. **Listen once with attention** — without doing anything else.
2. **Read the lyric** in Persian script.
3. **Identify the persona**: who is speaking? to whom? from what specific moment?
4. **Find the one line** — the line that, alone, would make you stop and listen. Mark it.
5. **Count syllables per chorus line**. Note the consistency or variation.
6. **Identify the metaphor system** — is it body? religion? food? city? weather? Does it stay in one system or switch?
7. **Note register shifts** — does it stay in one register, or swing فاخر ↔ محاوره within a single verse? Where, and why?
8. **Note the ending** — does the song fade, punch, repeat, ask a question, refuse resolution?

Doing this on 10 songs teaches more than 50 pages of theory.

## Artists worth studying, by technique

### محسن نامجو (Mohsen Namjoo) — register switching, body imagery, classical/modern collision

Listen for:

- Mid-line register jumps — classical Persian word in one half, slang in the other half. The collision is the point.
- Body parts and anatomical/medical metaphors used as emotional vehicles.
- Recurring rhythmic pulse even when meter breaks.
- Songs to study: **«ترانه‌ای دیگر برای کارگیسو»**, **«جبر جغرافیایی»**, **«شکر»**, **«سنه ده گالمیشم»**.

What to copy: the *willingness* to break register, not the surface vocabulary. Most imitators copy the dictionary; the technique is the swing.

### احمد شاملو (as lyricist, via Farhad and others) — image-first, plain syntax, no decoration

Listen for:

- Almost no overt rhyme; everything is held together by image and rhythm.
- Plain present-tense statements that carry massive weight because the imagery is precise.
- Songs to study: lyrics he wrote that Farhad sang — **«جمعه»**, **«چو ابر بهار»**.

What to copy: the discipline of saying one true thing simply. Cut every decorative adjective.

### اردلان سرفراز — narrative compression, cinematic verses

Listen for:

- Verses that read like film stills — a scene named in 4–6 syllables, no exposition.
- Choruses that distil the verse into one emotional pivot.
- Songs to study: **«دو پنجره»**, **«برکه»** (sung by Dariush).

What to copy: the ability to make a verse a scene, not a statement.

### شهیار قنبری — novelty of imagery, surreal precision

Listen for:

- Imagery you've never met in a Persian song before — a fish in a sentence, a clock with no hands, a bird that forgot its name.
- The willingness to surprise the listener every 2 lines.
- Songs to study: **«قصه دو ماهی»** (sung by Dariush), **«وای از این روزها»**.

What to copy: the imagination to find an image the language hasn't worn out yet.

### ایرج جنتی عطایی — social storytelling, restraint, narrator-as-witness

Listen for:

- The narrator as observer, not protagonist.
- Songs that tell a small story start to end, no abstract emotional flexing.
- Songs to study: **«بوی خوب گندم»** (sung by Dariush), **«هواخوری»**.

What to copy: how to let a story do the emotional work without naming the emotion.

### حسین صفا — modern density, layered metaphor, ambiguity

Listen for:

- Three metaphors fighting for the same syllable.
- Lines that mean two things at once and you have to pick.
- Songs to study: collaborations with Chavoshi (**«قاتل»** style) and Sirvan.

What to copy: how to compress without losing the through-line. (Bad imitators get only the density.)

### روزبه بمانی — modern emotional ballad, clean direct hook

Listen for:

- Choruses that work because they're emotionally direct, not because they're clever.
- Verses that build the case for the chorus's directness.
- Songs to study: **«چالوس»**, **«پشت چراغ قرمز»**.

What to copy: the willingness to be emotionally plain. Sometimes the angle is *that there is no angle, just feeling*.

### هیچ‌کس (Soroush Lashkari) — rap social conscience, internal rhyme, beat-locked phrasing

Listen for:

- Internal rhyme on every line, not just line ends.
- 2-syllable / 4-syllable phrasing that locks to the beat.
- Storytelling discipline — narrator, scene, conflict, no abstract anger.
- Songs to study: **«یه روز خوب میاد»**, **«صدای انتظار»**.

What to copy: the rhythmic phrasing and the documentary specificity. Don't copy the topical anger if your song isn't about that.

### ساسی مانکن (Sasy Mankan) — party-pop, hook density, register simplicity

Listen for:

- Choruses that are *fun before they are smart*. Specificity comes from party context (place, person, dance, car), not from inner emotion.
- Tehrangeles diction, English-loan-words, signature ad-libs.
- Songs to study: **«تهرون»**, **«جنده»** (controversial; for structural study only).

What to copy: how a low-conceptual song still has shape — hook, drop, ad-lib, repeat. Don't reach for emotional weight that the form can't carry.

### گوگوش (vocalist for many of the great lyricists) — declamation, clarity, line landing

Listen for:

- How the *delivery* makes a line land — emphasis on the right syllable, intentional breath.
- Songs to study: **«من و گنجشکای خونه»**, **«اعتراف»**.

What to copy (as a writer): write lyrics that *invite* this kind of delivery. Choose words that a great singer can lean into.

### داریوش — narrator-of-society, sustained delivery, large-arc lines

Listen for:

- The narrator addresses society or a generation, not a lover.
- Sentence shapes are long-arc; choruses are statements, not pleas.
- Songs to study: **«درد مشترک»**, **«جنگل»**.

What to copy: how to write a song with a public voice without becoming a sermon.

### حافظ مقدم — sparse, image-led, restraint as style

Listen for:

- 4–6 syllables per line; lots of white space.
- Single image per stanza, allowed to breathe.
- Songs to study: a small catalogue; seek the modern minimal Persian lyric tradition.

What to copy: the courage to let one image carry a verse. Stop adding when the verse is done.

## Cross-artist patterns to notice

After listening to 10+ tracks, you'll start to see:

- **The best Persian choruses are short** — 4 lines, often 7–10 syllables each. Long choruses lose the listener.
- **The best Persian verses are scenic** — they put you in a place, not in a feeling.
- **The best Persian lyrics commit to one persona** — even when the song uses metaphor, the speaker is one specific person.
- **The best Persian songs have one "stop" line** — a line that, if you hadn't been paying attention, makes you start.

## Anti-patterns common in AI-generated Persian

Cross-check your draft against this list. If you find these, you've drifted into the average:

- Cliché images stacked (شب، باران، دل، تنها in one verse).
- Persona absent — the voice could be anyone.
- Chorus that's a list of feelings, not a moment.
- "Romantic uncle wedding-toast" register — vaguely lofty, abstractly emotional, says nothing.
- Verse 2 is just verse 1 rephrased.
- Bridge says the same thing as the verses but louder.
- Final chorus is identical to first chorus with no earned escalation.

If your draft hits even three of these, run Step 7 (Red Team) again before delivering.
